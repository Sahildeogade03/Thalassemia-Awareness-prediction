Thalassemia

Thalassemia (thal-uh-SEE-me-uh) is an inherited blood disorder. It affects your body’s ability to produce normal hemoglobin. Hemoglobin is a protein in red blood cells. It allows your red blood cells to transport oxygen throughout your body, nourishing your body’s other cells.
If you have thalassemia, your body produces fewer healthy hemoglobin proteins, and your bone marrow produces fewer healthy red blood cells. The condition of having fewer red blood cells is called anemia. As red blood cells serve the vital role of delivering oxygen to tissues in your body, not having enough healthy red blood cells can deprive your body’s cells of the oxygen they need to make energy and thrive.

How does thalassemia affect body?

Thalassemia can cause mild or severe anemia and other complications over time (such as iron overload). Symptoms of anemia include:

  • Fatigue
  • Weakness
  • Facial bone deformities
  • Slow growth
  • Abdominal swelling
  • Trouble breathing
  • Feeling cold
  • Dizziness
  • Pale skin

Some babies show signs and symptoms of thalassemia at birth; others develop them during the first two years of life. Some people who have only one affected hemoglobin gene don't have thalassemia symptoms.

Causes of Thalassemia

Thalassemia is caused by mutations in the DNA of cells that make hemoglobin — the substance in red blood cells that carries oxygen throughout your body. The mutations associated with thalassemia are passed from parents to children.

In alpha-thalassemia, the severity of thalassemia depends on the number of gene mutations inherited from parents, affecting the production of alpha hemoglobin chains.

In beta-thalassemia, mutations affect the beta hemoglobin chain, with the severity varying based on which part of the hemoglobin molecule is affected.

For example:

  • Alpha-thalassemia:
    • One mutated gene: No symptoms, but a carrier.
    • Two mutated genes: Mild symptoms (alpha-thalassemia trait).
    • Three mutated genes: Moderate to severe symptoms.
    • Four mutated genes: Rare and severe, often resulting in stillbirth or severe complications.
  • Beta-thalassemia:
    • One mutated gene: Mild symptoms (thalassemia minor).
    • Two mutated genes: Moderate to severe symptoms (thalassemia major).

Symptoms of Thalassemia

Your experience will depend on the type of thalassemia you have and its severity:

  • Asymptomatic (no symptoms):
    • You likely won’t have symptoms if you’re missing one alpha gene. If you’re missing two alpha genes or one beta gene, you may be asymptomatic. Or, you may have mild anemia symptoms, like fatigue.
  • Mild to moderate symptoms:
    • Beta thalassemia intermedia may cause mild anemia symptoms, or it may cause the following symptoms associated with more moderate disease:
    • Growth problems.
    • Delayed puberty.
    • Bone abnormalities, such as osteoporosis.
    • An enlarged spleen (the organ in your abdomen that plays a part in fighting infection).
    • You may eventually need surgery to correct skeletal problems. Your healthcare provider may need to remove your spleen if it grows too large.
  • Severe symptoms:
    • Missing three alpha genes (Hemoglobin H disease) often causes anemia symptoms at birth and leads to severe lifelong anemia.
    • Beta thalassemia major (Cooley’s anemia) often leads to severe anemia symptoms noticeable by age 2.
    • Symptoms of severe anemia include those associated with mild to moderate disease. Additional symptoms may include:
    • Poor appetite.
    • Pale or yellowish skin (jaundice).
    • Urine that’s dark or tea-colored.
    • Irregular bone structure in your face.

Diagnosis and Tests for Thalassemia

Complete Blood Count (CBC)

Includes measures of hemoglobin and red blood cell quantity and size.

Reticulocyte Count

Measures young red blood cells to assess bone marrow activity.

Iron Studies

Helps differentiate between iron deficiency anemia and thalassemia.

Hemoglobin Electrophoresis

Specifically used to diagnose beta thalassemia by analyzing hemoglobin types.

Genetic Testing

Used to diagnose alpha thalassemia by identifying specific genetic mutations.

Additional Tests

Other tests may include bone marrow examination and fetal hemoglobin analysis.

Treatment of Thalassemia

Standard treatments for thalassemia major include:

  • Blood Transfusion: Involves receiving injections of red blood cells to restore normal levels of healthy red blood cells and hemoglobin.
  • Iron Chelation: Removal of excess iron from the body to prevent iron overload caused by frequent blood transfusions.
  • Folic Acid Supplements: Helps the body make healthy blood cells.
  • Bone Marrow Transplant: Cure for thalassemia; involves injecting bone marrow stem cells from a compatible donor.
  • Luspatercept: Injection to help the body make more red blood cells, approved for transfusion-dependent beta thalassemia.
